How the projection works

The 2023 reform fixed the Bundestag at 630 seats and abolished overhang and levelling seats, so a party's national total follows its second-vote share. Those seats are divided by the Sainte-Laguë/Schepers method among parties on at least 5%, or holding at least three constituencies, or recognised as a national minority party (the SSW). Each party's seats are then shared among its 16 Land lists by the same method.

Constituencies use uniform national swing. A party's move against its 2025 second-vote share is added, in percentage points, to its first-vote share in all 299 constituencies, floored at zero; whoever leads wins. Land second votes are swung the same way.

The constituency map shades all 299 Wahlkreise by projected first-vote winner, on the official 2025 boundaries; a hatched seat is one a party wins but can't fill for want of second-vote coverage in that Land. The Länder tab collapses the same data to 16 areas. Clicking either filters the breakdown table.

Direct counts constituency winners who actually take a seat. A win only converts if the party earned enough second votes in that Land to cover it, so where a party wins more constituencies than its Land contingent, the excess goes unfilled and nobody represents those seats. List is the remainder, filled from the Land list. At zero swing the model returns the official 2025 result exactly: 208 Union, 152 AfD, 120 SPD, 85 Grüne, 64 Linke, 1 SSW, with 23 constituencies unfilled.

Two caveats worth keeping in view. Swings are never truly uniform — they run stronger where a party is already strong, and this model understates that. And first votes are swung by second-vote movements, which glosses over the personal vote that carries Linke candidates in Berlin and keeps the CSU ahead of its list share. CDU and CSU take one combined input, since no voter chooses between them: the CSU stands only in Bavaria and the CDU only outside it. The model splits the bloc back apart on that geography and allocates the two as separate parties, which is what the law does.
