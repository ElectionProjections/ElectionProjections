How this projection works

Real local baseline. Every one of Scotland's 32 councils and roughly 356 wards is built from actual first‑preference results from the 5 May 2022 elections, gathered ward by ward. This captures genuine local variation — a party's national vote share moves differently in a Glasgow ward than in a rural Highland one, because it starts from a different place.

Uniform proportional swing. When you move a slider, each party's new national share is compared with its actual 2022 national share, and that ratio is applied to every ward where the party stood in 2022. So a party polling 10% above its 2022 figure sees its support scaled up roughly 10% in every ward it contested, not just added flatly — which avoids impossible negative shares and keeps the geography realistic.

A genuine STV count. Each ward is then counted using the Weighted Inclusive Gregory Method, the official method used for real Scottish council counts: candidates clearing the Droop quota are elected and their surplus transfers onward; if nobody clears quota, the lowest-placed is eliminated and their votes transfer. This repeats until every seat in the ward is filled.

Transfer assumptions. Scotland doesn't publish a single transfer matrix between parties, so each transfer step uses a model of where votes tend to go — calibrated to the well-documented pattern from 2022 and recent Scottish STV counts (analysed in detail by election-watchers like Ballot Box Scotland): Green and SNP voters' transfers cluster together and toward Labour, Conservative voters' transfers lean toward Liberal Democrats and independents, and a meaningful share of every transfer simply exhausts (the voter didn't rank enough candidates). These are reasonable, sourced assumptions, not a literal record of how every 2022 voter ranked their ballot.

One simplification worth knowing. To make a national tool feasible, each party is treated as a single transferable block per ward rather than tracking individual candidates. This reproduces real STV mechanics faithfully in the vast majority of wards, but in the rare case where a party's vote is heavily split between two unequal candidates in a ward, the model can occasionally award that party a seat that depended on local candidate strength rather than party strength alone. Independent candidates are the one place this matters most directly — every ward's independents are different people with no shared platform, so they're necessarily grouped per ward to run the count. Every other small party, by contrast, is tracked individually under its own name everywhere it stood in 2022. They don't get their own sliders; their combined "Other" total is calculated automatically as whatever's left once the six main parties are set, so the total always reaches 100%.

Reform UK didn't contest the 2022 election at all, so it has no real local result to scale up or down the way every other party does. Instead, each ward is given a notional "shape" built from a blend of that ward's real 2022 Conservative and Labour results (40% and 33% respectively) plus a touch of whoever else stood there — reflecting where Reform-type support tends to concentrate — and the slider scales that shape exactly the way every other party's slider scales its real 2022 number. At 0%, Reform contributes nothing anywhere, which is the actual 2022 result. Reform's transfer behaviour is also the least well-evidenced part of this model, since there's no 2022 data to draw on. It's instead built from Ballot Box Scotland's analysis of the 22 council by-elections held in 2025, which found Reform's voters favoured their own party over Lib Dem, Labour, Green and SNP voters by margins of roughly 2–3 to 1, but over Conservative voters by only 1.5 to 1 — Reform is markedly less unwelcome to Conservative voters than to anyone else's. Those ratios were used to carve a Reform share out of each party's existing transfer pattern, shrinking everything else proportionally rather than guessing a whole new set of numbers from scratch.
